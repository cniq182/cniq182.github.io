A City in Flux

Deciphering San Francisco's crime metamorphosis during the pandemic years.

San Francisco is a city of contrasts, where the tech boom meets deep-rooted social challenges. When the 2020 pandemic hit, the city’s streets—usually teeming with tourists and commuters—became silent. However, data from the SF Open Data Portal reveals that crime did not simply stop; it transformed.

In this short story, we explore how specific focus crimes evolved, where they concentrated, and why we must be careful when interpreting these patterns as pure "criminality."

The Geographic Paradox

While the overall city volume changed, the "hotspots" remained stubbornly anchored to specific neighborhoods. As we discussed in class regarding the Richardson et al. paper, crime data is often a reflection of police activity rather than just incident occurrence. This is particularly visible in districts like the Tenderloin.

Rhythms of the Street

Crime also has a pulse. By looking at the hourly distribution, we can see how the "business hours" of crime shifted. The transition from a nightlife-heavy crime profile to one dominated by daylight incidents reflects a city that had lost its 24-hour rhythm.

Conclusion & Reflection

Looking at these visualizations, it is tempting to conclude that SF became "safer" or "more dangerous" at different points. However, as data storytellers, we must acknowledge the limitations. A drop in "Drug Offenses" might reflect a policy change in the DA's office or a shift in police patrols rather than a reduction in substance use. This project serves as a reminder that behind every data point, there is a complex social reality that a single chart cannot fully capture.
